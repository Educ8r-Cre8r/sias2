This photograph features the seed heads of an ornamental grass, likely Fountain Grass (\*Pennisetum\*), covered in a dense layer of morning dew. The tiny, clear water droplets are clinging to the fine, hair-like bristles (setae) of the plant. The background shows blurred green blades of grass, indicating a lush, moist environment. The interaction between the water and the plant surface highlights surface tension, as the droplets maintain a spherical shape rather than soaking immediately into the plant tissue.

\---

\#\# üî¨ Scientific Phenomena

The anchoring phenomenon is \*\*Condensation and the Water Cycle\*\*. During the night, the surface of the grass cools down. When the temperature of the plant surface drops below the "dew point" of the surrounding air, water vapor (a gas) in the air loses thermal energy and transforms into liquid water. This demonstrates how matter changes state based on temperature shifts within a local ecosystem.

\---

\#\# üìö Core Science Concepts

1\.  \*\*Phase Changes (States of Matter):\*\* The transition of water from a gas (water vapor) to a liquid (dew) due to cooling.   
2\.  \*\*Environmental Interactions:\*\* How plants provide surfaces for water collection, which can benefit the plant and local micro-organisms.  
3\.  \*\*Properties of Matter:\*\* The way water forms droplets illustrates cohesive forces and surface tension, allowing it to "bead up" on the bristles.

\<pedagogical-tip\>  
When discussing dew, avoid the term "sweating." Students often think plants "sweat" like humans. Instead, use a cold soda can or a mirror in a bathroom to show that the water comes from the air, not from inside the object itself. This helps move students from an internal-source theory to an atmospheric-source theory.  
\</pedagogical-tip\>

\<udl-suggestions\>  
\- \*\*Tactile Exploration:\*\* If possible, bring in a dew-covered plant or use a spray mister on faux fur to let students feel the "bristles" and droplets.  
\- \*\*Visual Modeling:\*\* Use blue "pom-poms" to represent water vapor in the air that "sticks" to a pipe cleaner (the grass) when it gets "cold."  
\- \*\*Time-Lapse Media:\*\* Provide a time-lapse video of dew forming or evaporating to support students who struggle with static images of dynamic processes.  
\</udl-suggestions\>

\#\# üîç Zoom In / Zoom Out Concepts

1\.  \*\*Zoom In:\*\* At the molecular level, water vapor molecules in the air are moving rapidly. As they hit the cool surface of the grass, they slow down and stick together through hydrogen bonding, forming the liquid droplets seen in the photo.  
2\.  \*\*Zoom Out:\*\* This represents a tiny part of the \*\*Earth's Water Cycle\*\*. The dew will eventually evaporate back into the atmosphere as the sun warms the air, contributing to humidity and cloud formation.

\#\# ‚ö†Ô∏è Potential Student Misconceptions

1\.  \*\*Misconception:\*\* It rained last night.  
    \* \*\*Clarification:\*\* Dew forms even on clear nights when the sky is cloudless; the water comes from the invisible gas in the air around us.  
2\.  \*\*Misconception:\*\* The plant "leaked" the water.  
    \* \*\*Clarification:\*\* While plants do release water through transpiration, the "beads" on the outside are primarily atmospheric condensation.  
3\.  \*\*Misconception:\*\* Dew only happens on grass.  
    \* \*\*Clarification:\*\* Dew can form on spider webs, car windshields, and stones, proving it is an atmospheric process rather than a biological one.

\#\# üí¨ Discussion Questions

\#\# üìñ Science Vocabulary

\* \*\*Condensation:\*\* The process by which a gas changes into a liquid.  
\* \*\*Water Vapor:\*\* Water in its invisible, gaseous state.  
\* \*\*Dew Point:\*\* The temperature at which air becomes saturated and water vapor condenses into liquid.  
\* \*\*Evaporation:\*\* The process by which a liquid changes into a gas.  
\* \*\*Surface Tension:\*\* The property of a liquid's surface that allows it to resist an external force.
