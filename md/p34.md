\#\# üìö Core Concepts

1\.  \*\*Phase Change:\*\* The transformation of a substance from one state of matter to another. In this case, water (liquid) changes to ice (solid).  
2\.  \*\*Freezing:\*\* The phase change from a liquid to a solid. This happens when a liquid loses enough thermal energy (heat) to reach its freezing point.  
3\.  \*\*Temperature:\*\* A measure of the average kinetic energy of particles in a substance. When water freezes, its temperature is at or below 0¬∞C (32¬∞F).  
4\.  \*\*Water Cycle:\*\* This image shows a part of the water cycle where liquid water (from melted snow or ice) freezes into solid ice. This is a natural process that occurs in colder climates.

\<pedagogical-tip\>  
To make the concept of freezing tangible, have students conduct a simple experiment. Provide them with a bowl of water and a freezer. Have them record the temperature of the water before placing it in the freezer and then check it every 10 minutes until it freezes. This hands-on activity allows students to observe the temperature change and the physical transformation of water into ice, reinforcing the concept of phase change.  
\</pedagogical-tip\>

\#\# üéì Zoom In / Zoom Out Concepts

1\.  \*\*Zoom In:\*\* At the molecular level, the water molecules in a liquid are constantly moving and sliding past each other. When the temperature drops below 0¬∞C, the molecules lose kinetic energy and move more slowly. They begin to form stable hydrogen bonds, arranging themselves into a rigid, hexagonal lattice structure, which is the solid form of ice.  
2\.  \*\*Zoom Out:\*\* This image is a part of the larger water cycle. The water that formed these icicles likely originated as snowfall. The snow melted due to warmer temperatures, became liquid water, and then froze again when the temperature dropped below freezing. This process is a natural part of weather patterns and climate in many regions.

\#\# üö´ Potential Student Misconceptions

1\.  \*\*Misconception:\*\* Icicles form by snow freezing on a roof.  
    \*\*Clarification:\*\* Icicles form from water, not snow. The snow melts first, and then the meltwater drips down and freezes.  
2\.  \*\*Misconception:\*\* Icicles are made of ice crystals.  
    \*\*Clarification:\*\* While ice is made of water molecules, the visible icicle is a solid mass of frozen water. The term "crystal" can be misleading; icicles are a solid form of water, not a collection of separate crystals.  
3\.  \*\*Misconception:\*\* Icicles only form when it's snowing.  
    \*\*Clarification:\*\* Icicles form when temperatures are below freezing, but they can form after snow has melted. They require a source of liquid water and cold air.
